# User Persona: Maya Reyes

**Persona Type:** Environmentally and Community Focused Cannabis User
**Last Updated:** April 2026

---

## Photo Description

Maya is 31, Dominican-Puerto Rican, with box braids usually pulled back with a bandana. She's photographed at a community garden in the South Bronx, dirt on her knees, laughing at something off-camera. She's wearing a vintage oversized tee from a local screen printer, wide-leg linen pants, and beat-up Saucony Jazz sneakers. There's a tote bag slung over her shoulder with a bodega flower sticking out. She looks like she just got back from somewhere good and is on her way to somewhere better.

---

## 1. Demographics

- **Age:** 28-35
- **Location:** Brooklyn (Bushwick/Bed-Stuy), but could just as easily be Ridgewood, the South Bronx, or upstate in Hudson/Kingston. She has friends scattered across the five boroughs and takes Metro North upstate on weekends sometimes.
- **Household Income:** $55K-$80K. She's not broke but she's not comfortable either. She's strategic about where her money goes.
- **Education:** Bachelor's degree (CUNY system -- maybe Hunter or City College). Might have some grad school credits she never finished. Educated but not credentialed in that elite-university way.
- **Occupation:** Works in the nonprofit/social enterprise world. Could be a program coordinator at an environmental justice org, a community garden manager, or doing communications for a social equity cannabis company. The job matters to her. She turned down better-paying gigs because the mission wasn't right.
- **Living Situation:** Rents a 2BR with a roommate in a walk-up. Has plants everywhere. The fire escape has herbs growing on it that are technically against the lease.
- **Relationship:** Has a partner, or doesn't -- it's not the organizing principle of her life. Her community is.

---

## 2. Psychographics

**Core Values:**
- Reciprocity over charity. She doesn't want to "help" communities, she wants to be in community.
- Environmental stewardship as a daily practice, not a brand identity. She composts. She brings her own containers. She does it because it's right, not because anyone is watching.
- Authenticity over aesthetics. She can smell performative sustainability from across the room.
- Local economies matter. She'd rather pay more at a place she trusts than save money at a place that exploits.

**Beliefs About Cannabis:**
- Cannabis is a plant, not a vice. She has zero patience for stigma.
- The war on drugs devastated her community. She's aware of the racial history and it shapes every purchasing decision.
- She believes the legal market should benefit the people who were harmed by prohibition. If it doesn't, it's just colonization with a license.
- She sees cannabis as medicine, creativity tool, social ritual, and spiritual practice depending on the day.

**Attitudes:**
- Skeptical of corporate cannabis. She watched the big MSOs move into New York and it made her angry.
- Protective of small operators. She roots for the underdog because she is one.
- Not preachy about it, though. She's not lecturing at parties. She just quietly makes choices and if you ask, she'll tell you why.

---

## 3. Cannabis Consumption Habits

**What She Buys:**
- Flower first. Always flower. She appreciates the ritual of grinding, rolling, smelling it. Hash rosin when she wants to treat herself.
- She gravitates toward organic, sun-grown, living soil -- even if the packaging doesn't say it in those exact words. She can tell by the smoke.
- She'll buy a half-eighth or a gram of rosin rather than stocking up. Small purchases, high intention.

**How She Consumes:**
- Rolls joints. Occasionally uses a small glass piece at home. She's not into heavy hardware -- no dab rigs, no vape pens with batteries. She likes things simple and close to the plant.
- Smokes socially on a stoop or a rooftop with friends. Smokes alone at night while journaling or drawing.
- Micro-doses edibles for anxiety sometimes but prefers flower because she likes controlling the experience.

**What Matters To Her:**
1. **Source and process.** Who grew this? How? She wants to know the hands that touched it.
2. **Clean inputs.** No PGRs, no synthetic nutrients, no pesticides. Living soil is the standard.
3. **Flavor and effect over THC percentage.** She thinks the THC number race is stupid. She wants complex terpene profiles and a high that feels clean.
4. **Sustainability of the operation.** Is the grow using LED or HPS? Are they wasting water? Is the packaging recyclable? She notices.
5. **Price matters, but not most.** She'll pay $50-60 for an eighth she believes in before she pays $35 for corporate mids. But she has a ceiling. She's not paying boutique prices every time.

## 4. Media & Digital Behavior

**Social Platforms:**
- **Instagram** is her primary discovery tool. She follows growers, hash makers, community gardens, and local artists. She watches Stories more than she scrolls the feed. She engages with small accounts -- comments, shares to her close friends story.
- **TikTok** for entertainment and learning. She watches living soil grow content, composting videos, and cannabis education creators. She doesn't post much but she saves videos constantly.
- **Twitter/X** -- she still checks it for cannabis policy news and local politics but she's mostly off it emotionally.
- **Substack/newsletters** -- she subscribes to a few. Cannabis industry analysis, environmental justice writing, local food systems content.
- **Reddit** -- lurks r/NYCcannabis, r/livingsoil, r/rosin. Never posts. Reads everything.

**Content She Engages With:**
- Behind-the-scenes grow content. She wants to see the soil, the plants, the process.
- Founder stories, especially from BIPOC operators. She wants to know who you are before she cares what you sell.
- Educational content that doesn't talk down to her. She knows more than most budtenders.
- Community event promotion. Pop-ups, sesh events, farmers markets, art shows.
- She does NOT engage with: hype drops, "fire emoji" marketing, bro-science content, anything that feels like it's cosplaying street culture.

**How She Discovers New Brands:**
- A friend puts her on. Word of mouth is everything.
- She sees it at a dispensary she already trusts.
- She finds the grower's personal Instagram before she finds the brand account.
- She reads about it in a cannabis newsletter or local publication (Gothamist, Hell Gate, The City).

---

## 5. Pain Points

- **Corporate cannabis dominating shelf space.** She walks into dispensaries and sees the same MSO brands everywhere. It makes her feel like the legal market failed.
- **Lack of transparency.** Most brands don't tell you how the plant was grown, what inputs were used, or who the grower is. It's all vibes and packaging with nothing behind it.
- **Greenwashing.** Brands slapping "organic" and "sustainable" on products without substance. She knows "organic" isn't even a legal term in cannabis and it irritates her when brands use it loosely.
- **Price vs. quality mismatch.** She's been burned paying premium prices for mid-quality flower that had good branding.
- **Social equity as a marketing buzzword.** She's seen companies use social equity language in their branding while doing nothing for the communities they claim to represent.
- **The legacy market was better in some ways.** Her old plug cared about the product. Some dispensaries feel like buying weed at a CVS.
- **Packaging waste.** The amount of plastic in legal cannabis packaging genuinely bothers her. She's thought about this more than once while standing at a dispensary counter.

---

## 6. What Draws Her to Wurmz

- **Living soil is the whole point.** Maya understands what living soil means -- it's not just a cultivation method, it's a philosophy. The soil is alive. The plant is an expression of the soil. When Wurmz says living soil, she knows they're not faking it because that's not something you can fake at scale.
- **Single source, seed to sale.** They grow the flower, wash the hash, press the rosin. All in-house, all one team. That chain of custody matters to her. She doesn't want to buy hash that was made from someone else's trim.
- **Micro license means small.** Small means accountable. Small means you can see who's behind it. She trusts small.
- **Craft quality she can verify.** Hand-trimmed, small batch, natural inputs. These aren't marketing claims to Maya -- they're things she can confirm with her eyes and nose when she opens the jar.
- **The aesthetic is honest.** Graffiti and street art is the visual language of New York. It's not focus-grouped. It's not trying to look like a wellness brand or a luxury brand. It looks like the city she lives in.
- **They're not pretending to be something they're not.** No corporate polish. No VC money vibes. No "we're disrupting the industry" language. They grow weed, and they grow it right.

## 7. Brand Touchpoints

**First Encounter:**
- A friend passes her a joint at a rooftop gathering and she asks what it is. The friend shows her the Wurmz bag. She takes a photo of it.
- She sees Wurmz on the shelf at a dispensary she trusts (Housing Works, a BIPOC-owned shop in her neighborhood) and the packaging catches her eye because it doesn't look like everything else.
- A grower she follows on Instagram reposts Wurmz content.

**What Makes Her Follow:**
- She checks the Instagram. If the content is mostly grow process, soil shots, wash day videos, and real moments -- not just product glamour shots -- she follows.
- If the brand engages with its community in the comments, not just posting and ghosting, she notices.
- If she sees the faces behind the brand, she stays.

**What Makes Her Buy:**
- She tries it once and the quality speaks. Clean burn, good flavor, smooth high that doesn't leave her foggy. That's the conversion moment.
- Seeing it in a dispensary she already shops at (availability + trust).
- Price within her range for what it is. She'll pay craft prices if the craft is real.
- Knowing the story. If she reads a short interview or sees a video about how and why Wurmz does what they do, that context makes the purchase feel like a choice, not just a transaction.

**What Makes Her a Repeat Customer:**
- Consistency. The second purchase is as good as the first.
- Feeling like she's supporting something real. Every purchase feels like a vote.
- New drops that show growth and experimentation, not just the same SKU in new packaging.
- If Wurmz shows up at community events, pop-ups, or farmers markets -- she deepens her loyalty when she meets the people.

## 8. Messaging That Resonates

**Language and Tone:**
- Direct. No fluff. Say what it is.
- Confident but not arrogant. You know your product is good, you don't need to shout about it.
- Use the language of growers, not marketers. "Living soil," "hand-trimmed," "full melt," "small batch" -- these are terms she already knows and respects.
- A little bit of New York edge. Not forced slang, but the cadence and energy of someone who actually lives here.

**Themes That Connect:**
- "Know your grower." Transparency as a core value.
- "From the soil up." The process is the product.
- "Small batch because we give a damn." Craft as intentional constraint, not limitation.
- "Real weed for real people." Unpretentious quality.
- Community over competition. We're all in this together.

**What to Avoid:**
- Wellness-brand language. Don't say "curated experience" or "elevated lifestyle." She will disengage immediately.
- Performative wokeness. Don't lead with social equity talking points unless you're backing it up with action. She'll check.
- "Premium" and "luxury" framing. She doesn't want weed that's trying to be champagne. She wants weed that's trying to be the best weed.
- Aggressive hype culture. All-caps drops, countdown timers, "limited edition" pressure. That's not her speed.
- Infantilizing education. Don't explain what terpenes are. She already knows. Talk to her like a peer.

---

## 9. Community Values

**Supporting Local:**
- She shops at local dispensaries over chains. She asks budtenders what's grown in New York.
- She goes to cannabis farmers markets and pop-up events. She shakes hands with growers.
- She'll promote a brand she likes by word of mouth without anyone asking. If it's good, she puts people on.

**Sustainability:**
- She evaluates brands on their actual practices, not their claims. Regenerative soil, minimal packaging, energy-efficient operations -- she notices all of it.
- She'd love to see cannabis brands partner with community gardens, composting programs, or environmental justice orgs. That would make her a customer for life.
- She saves jars and reuses containers. She'd be thrilled if a brand offered a return/refill program.

**Social Equity:**
- She supports brands with BIPOC ownership not because it's trendy but because she understands the historical context.
- She pays attention to who's getting licenses and who's getting shut out. She reads about it. She has opinions.
- She sees micro licenses as the mechanism that was supposed to keep cannabis local and equitable. When a brand actually operates on a micro license and does it well, that's meaningful to her.
- She wants to see cannabis revenue circulate in the communities that were harmed. If Wurmz hires locally, sources locally, shows up locally -- that's not marketing to her, that's alignment.

## 10. A Day in the Life

Maya wakes up at 7:15 in her Bushwick apartment to the sound of the M train rattling past. Her room is small but organized -- a few plants on the windowsill, a stack of books on the nightstand, a poster from a Bronx art collective taped to the wall.

She makes coffee in a moka pot, standing in the kitchen in socks, scrolling Instagram stories while she waits for it to brew. She double-taps a video of someone's outdoor living soil bed upstate. She saves a post from a hash maker in the Hudson Valley showing wash day -- the trichome heads floating in the water look unreal. She screenshots it and sends it to her friend Janelle with no caption.

She bikes to work at an environmental justice nonprofit in Hunts Point. The morning is meetings and grant reports. At lunch she walks to a taco truck with a coworker and they talk about a cannabis farmers market happening in Brooklyn this weekend. She makes a mental note.

After work she swings by the dispensary on her way home. She's looking for something specific -- small batch New York flower, living soil if she can find it. The budtender shows her a new drop. She picks up the jar, smells it, reads the label. "Who grows this?" she asks. The budtender doesn't know. She puts it back. She grabs a jar of Wurmz instead -- she knows the name, she's seen the grows on Instagram, and last time the Gush Mints was one of the cleanest smokes she's had in months. She gets an eighth.

She gets home, changes clothes, waters her plants. Her roommate is making dinner -- rice and beans, the apartment smells good. She rolls a joint on the fire escape with the Wurmz flower, takes two pulls, and watches the sun drop behind the Bushwick rooftops. The high comes on slow and warm. Her shoulders drop. She can taste the terps -- something sweet and gassy, like fruit left in the sun.

After dinner she sits on the couch with her sketchbook and draws for an hour. Nothing serious, just shapes and letters. Her phone buzzes -- Janelle responded to the hash video: "we need to go to that farm." Maya sends back a string of hand emojis.

She brushes her teeth, reads a few pages of a book about soil ecology, and falls asleep by 11. Tomorrow is Thursday. Friday her block is having a cleanup and she signed up to help. Saturday is the farmers market. She's bringing cash.

## Summary

Maya isn't an edge case. She represents a real and growing segment of New York cannabis consumers who care deeply about how their weed is grown, who benefits from their purchase, and whether the brand behind it is real or just performing. She's informed, skeptical, and loyal once she trusts you. She doesn't need to be convinced that craft cannabis is worth it -- she already believes that. She needs to be shown that Wurmz lives up to what it claims. And if it does, she'll do the marketing for you.
